There is a lot of interest in building world models into robot policies. Before asking why, it helps to be precise about what we mean. For robotics, a dynamics model is a specific form of world model1: \(p(s'|s,a)\), where \(a\) is the robot action. World model is used more broadly. In video generation, for instance, the action \(a\) can be a language prompt. Here I mean the robotics version, predicting the next state from the current state and the robot's action. Why build one into a policy? There are a few common reasons.

  • Planning: a dynamics model lets you look ahead before acting and spend extra compute per step.
  • Representation learning: fitting dynamics steers the model to capture how the world changes, not just which action to output.
  • Generality: dynamics is broader than predicting the action for one task. Random, suboptimal, and optimal data all teach how the world works, so all of it is useful for learning.

But a model-free policy can pick up each of these implicitly. Compute can go straight into action prediction by stacking more layers or recursive loops, instead of routing through a dynamics model. Given enough data, the network may learn good enough representations, and the latent may already capture the effect before it predicts the final action. Suboptimal data can be folded into action prediction by conditioning on the reward.2 None of these justifies why learning a dynamics or world model is a must for robotics.

The one thing that justifies learning dynamics to me is tapping into human videos.3 A human video is only states: we never see the actions. You could argue that the actions can be extracted, either by a heuristic or by learning latent actions. But a heuristic like "the state k steps ahead is the action" is not fundamentally true and clearly mismatches robot actions, which are truly actions and not state disguised as actions. And the principled route, learning latent actions,4 has to learn a dynamics model anyway. So it can be argued to fall into the bucket of learning a dynamics model, but a specific way of folding it into policy learning.

World-Action-Models (WAMs)5 are one way to fold it in. Why do it this way, and what it buys us, is what I want to work out by reading through these papers. I will come back to that at the end.

For each work below, I am not aiming to summarize the whole paper. I discuss the things specific to that paper and the main points I found interesting.

Cosmos Policy: Fine-Tuning Video Models for Visuomotor Control and Planning6

The policy starts from a pretrained video model. Instead of only predicting the action \(a\), it predicts three things at once: the action \(a\), the future observation \(s'\), and its value \(V(s')\). The future observation is predicted from the language, the current observation, and the action. The value is predicted from the full prefix, all of \(s\), \(a\), and \(s'\).

Two parts are interesting to me.

1. Adding new modalities without changing the architecture. The Cosmos model is only trained on latent frames. Robot policies need modalities it never saw during pretraining: proprioception, the action chunk, and the state value (scalar). The authors add these without any architectural change by making each modality look like a (latent) frame. A low-dimensional vector is normalized to \([-1, 1]\), tiled to fill a latent volume the size of one frame, and inserted into the sequence in the order \((s, a, s', V(s'))\): current observation, action chunk, future observation, value.

The same frozen layout then serves three roles, chosen by which frames are clean inputs and which are denoised as outputs. Denoise everything after \(s\) and it is a policy; denoise \(s'\) from \((s, a)\) and it is a world model; denoise only \(V(s')\) from \((s, a, s')\) and it is a value function. Nothing is added to the base model, and the same denoising objective trains all three.

The choice to add no new architecture rests on simplicity and on reusing the pretrained prior. The evidence does not compare latent-frame injection against an added module with the backbone and data held fixed, so it is unclear whether not adding new modules is better or worse.

2. Using the future prediction at test time. The future prediction is not only a training signal. At test time, the model samples several action candidates, imagines the future observation each one leads to, scores those futures with a value conditioned on the future observation alone, and executes the best. This is Best-of-N sampling. The gain from the sampling alone is not clear from the paper, but the combined benefit of fine-tuning on the rollout dataset and then ranking with Best-of-N is 12.5 points on the real-world ALOHA tasks.

So Cosmos Policy touches all three reasons from the start. It leans on the pretrained prior, and dropping it costs 3.9 points on LIBERO. It uses a world model for model-based planning, and folds in suboptimal rollout data through the same objective. But the gains are hard to read cleanly: the planning benefit comes bundled with extra rollout fine-tuning, together giving 12.5 points on the two real-world ALOHA tasks.
